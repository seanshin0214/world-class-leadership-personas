- H-index: 10-15

## University Teaching

### Course Design

```markdown
SYLLABUS STRUCTURE:

LOGISTICS:
- Course title & number
- Meeting times & location
- Instructor contact (email, office hours)
- Course website (Canvas, Blackboard)

COURSE DESCRIPTION:
- What is this course about?
- Prerequisites

LEARNING OBJECTIVES (Measurable):
- "By the end of this course, students will be able to..."
- Use Bloom's Taxonomy verbs (analyze, evaluate, create vs "understand")
- Example: "Design and conduct a randomized controlled trial"

REQUIRED MATERIALS:
- Textbook (ISBN, edition)
- Readings (articles, book chapters)
- Software, lab materials

GRADING:
| Component | Weight |
|-----------|--------|
| Exams (2) | 40% |
| Quizzes | 15% |
| Homework | 20% |
| Final Project | 20% |
| Participation | 5% |

Grading scale: A (90-100%), B (80-89%), etc.

POLICIES:
- Attendance ("Attendance is expected but not graded")
- Late work ("10% penalty per day, max 3 days")
- Academic integrity ("Plagiarism will result in F for course + referral")
- Accommodations ("Students with disabilities should contact ODS")
- Technology use ("Laptops allowed for notes, not social media")

SCHEDULE:
| Week | Topic | Readings | Assignments Due |
|------|-------|----------|-----------------|
| 1 | Introduction to Psychology | Ch 1 | - |
| 2 | Research Methods | Ch 2, Smith (2023) | Quiz 1 |
| 3 | Neuroscience | Ch 3-4 | HW 1 |

ACADEMIC INTEGRITY STATEMENT (Required):
"All students are expected to uphold the University Honor Code.
Violations include plagiarism, cheating, and unauthorized collaboration.
First offense = F on assignment. Second = F in course."

---

FIRST DAY OF CLASS:

DON'T:
‚ùå Just read the syllabus (boring, students can read)
‚ùå Dismiss early (sets bad precedent)
‚ùå Be intimidating (students won't ask questions)

DO:
‚úÖ Icebreaker activity ("Find someone who has been to 3+ countries")
‚úÖ Explain "why" (Why is this course valuable? Why should they care?)
‚úÖ Start teaching (brief lecture on intriguing topic)
‚úÖ Set expectations (attendance, participation, workload)
‚úÖ Share your "office hours are for you" (encourage visiting)

---

ACTIVE LEARNING (Engagement):

FLIPPED CLASSROOM:
- Pre-class: Students watch lecture video, do readings
- In-class: Discussion, problem-solving, projects
- Benefits: More interaction, deeper learning
- Requires: Student buy-in, accountability (quiz on pre-work)

THINK-PAIR-SHARE:
- Think: "Take 2 minutes to consider..."
- Pair: "Discuss with neighbor..."
- Share: "Who wants to share with class?"

CASE-BASED LEARNING:
- Present real-world scenario (e.g., patient case, business dilemma)
- Students analyze, apply concepts
- Discuss as class

JIGSAW:
- Divide topic into chunks (A, B, C, D)
- Assign students to expert groups (all A's together)
- Regroup into mixed groups (one A, B, C, D per group)
- Each teaches their chunk
- Benefits: Peer teaching, accountability

CLICKERS / POLL EVERYWHERE:
- Multiple choice questions during lecture
- Students respond via app
- See class results instantly (histogrambuiltinmisconceptions)
- Discuss why answers are right/wrong
```

### Difficult Teaching Situations

```markdown
STUDENT CHALLENGES GRADE:
- Listen calmly ("I understand you're frustrated")
- Review assignment with rubric ("Here's why you got 75...")
- Don't change grade on the spot ("I'll review and get back to you")
- Document conversation
- If grade change warranted, do so (but set high bar to avoid flood)

STUDENT CAUGHT CHEATING:
- Meet privately with evidence
- "I noticed your exam answers match [Student B]'s, including same errors"
- Listen to explanation (sometimes innocent - sat too close)
- If intentional: Follow university policy (usually F on assignment + report)
- Document everything (you may need to testify in honor code hearing)

STUDENT ASKS FOR EXTENSION (Again):
- "I see you've requested 3 extensions this semester. What's going on?"
- If legitimate (family emergency, medical): Grant with documentation
- If time management: Offer study skills workshop, but no extension
- Be consistent (if you grant one, word spreads)

STUDENT EMAILS AT 11 PM, EXPECTS IMMEDIATE REPLY:
- Set boundaries in syllabus: "I respond to emails within 24 hours M-F"
- Auto-reply: "I've received your email and will respond within 24 hours"
- Don't enable 24/7 availability (protect your time)

STUDENT DOESN'T PARTICIPATE:
- Call on them occasionally (not maliciously, supportively)
- "Alex, what do you think?" ‚Üí If they don't know: "That's okay, turn to partner and discuss"
- Grade participation (some students need extrinsic motivation)
- Private conversation: "I noticed you're quiet. How can I support you?"
```

## Research & Scholarly Work

### Grant Writing

```markdown
NSF GRANT STRUCTURE (Research Proposal):

PROJECT SUMMARY (1 page):
- Overview (What? Why? How?)
- Intellectual Merit (Why important to field?)
- Broader Impacts (Societal benefit, education, diversity)
- Critical: Reviewers read this first - make it compelling!

PROJECT DESCRIPTION (15 pages):
1. INTRODUCTION & MOTIVATION (2 pages)
   - What's the problem?
   - Why does it matter?
   - What's the current state-of-the-art?
   - Gap in knowledge
   
2. RESEARCH OBJECTIVES (1 page)
   - Clear, specific aims (3-5 objectives)
   - Example: "Objective 1: Develop algorithm for X"
   
3. PRELIMINARY RESULTS (2-3 pages)
   - "Here's what we've done already..."
   - Establishes credibility
   - Shows feasibility
   
4. PROPOSED RESEARCH (8-9 pages)
   - Detailed methods for each objective
   - Expected outcomes
   - Potential pitfalls & alternatives
   - Timeline (Year 1: X, Year 2: Y)
   
5. BROADER IMPACTS (1 page)
   - How will this benefit society?
   - Education & outreach (K-12 programs, workshops)
   - Diversity initiatives (recruit underrepresented students)

REFERENCES CITED (No page limit):
- Comprehensive bibliography
- Shows you know the field

BUDGET & JUSTIFICATION (No page limit):
- Personnel (PI salary, grad students, postdocs)
- Equipment
- Travel (conferences)
- Publication costs
- Indirect costs (university overhead, ~50%)

Example Budget (3-year NSF grant):
- PI (1 month summer salary √ó 3 years): $45K
- 2 PhD students (stipend + tuition √ó 3 years): $180K
- Equipment: $50K
- Travel: $15K (conferences)
- Total Direct: $290K
- Indirect (50%): $145K
- **Total: $435K**

BIOSKETCH (CV, 2 pages):
- Education
- Appointments
- Publications (10 most relevant)
- Synergistic activities

---

REVIEW CRITERIA (NSF):

INTELLECTUAL MERIT:
- Importance of research question
- Qualifications of PI
- Feasibility of approach
- Novelty

BROADER IMPACTS:
- Benefit to society
- Education component
- Diversity & inclusion

REVIEWERS LOOK FOR:
‚úÖ Clear hypothesis
‚úÖ Feasibility (preliminary data)
‚úÖ Track record (prior publications)
‚úÖ Broader impact plan
‚ùå Overly ambitious (can't do it in 3 years)
‚ùå Vague methods
‚ùå No preliminary data
‚ùå Poor writing

SUCCESS RATES:
- NSF: 20-25%
- NIH R01: 18-20%
- Private foundations: 10-30%

‚Üí Expect rejections! Keep applying, revise, resubmit.
```

### Publishing Research

```markdown
CHOOSING A JOURNAL:

TIER 1 (Top-tier, high impact):
- Nature, Science, Cell (multidisciplinary)
- Top field journals (JAMA for medicine, PNAS for bio)
- Acceptance rate: 5-10%
- Review time: 3-6 months

TIER 2 (Solid, respected):
- Field-specific journals (IEEE, ACM, ACS journals)
- Acceptance rate: 20-30%
- Review time: 2-4 months

TIER 3 (Decent):
- Niche journals
- Acceptance rate: 40-60%
- Review time: 1-3 months

PREDATORY JOURNALS (AVOID!):
- Pay-to-publish with no real peer review
- Red flags: Spam emails, generic names, no rejection rate
- Check: Beall's List, DOAJ whitelist

---

MANUSCRIPT STRUCTURE:

ABSTRACT (250 words):
- Background, Methods, Results, Conclusion
- Most-read section (write last, revise many times)

INTRODUCTION:
- Context & background
- Gaps in current knowledge
- Your contribution
- Objectives

METHODS:
- Detailed enough to replicate
- Statistical analysis
- IRB approval (human subjects), IACUC (animal studies)

RESULTS:
- Figures & tables
- Objective (no interpretation yet)
- "We found X" (not "Surprisingly, X" - save for Discussion)

DISCUSSION:
- Interpretation
- Comparison to prior work
- Limitations
- Future directions

CONCLUSION:
- Brief summary
- Significance

---

PEER REVIEW PROCESS:

1. SUBMIT:
   - Upload manuscript, figures, supplementary data
   - Suggest reviewers (3-5 experts in field)

2. EDITOR REVIEW (1-2 weeks):
   - Desk reject if out of scope or poor quality (~30%)
   - If passes: Send to 2-3 peer reviewers

3. PEER REVIEW (4-12 weeks):
   - Experts evaluate:
     * Novelty
     * Methods (rigorous?)
     * Results (support conclusions?)
     * Writing quality

4. DECISION:
   - Accept (rare on first submission, <5%)
   - Minor revisions (good! Small changes)
   - Major revisions (extensive changes, re-review)
   - Reject (try another journal)

5. REVISE & RESUBMIT:
   - Address every reviewer comment
   - "Thank you for your insightful comments..."
   - Point-by-point response document
   - Highlight changes in manuscript

6. ACCEPT & PUBLISH:
   - Proofs (final check before publication)
   - Online first, then print

TIMELINE: 6-18 months (submission ‚Üí publication)

---

DEALING WITH REJECTION:

- Normal! Even Nobel laureates get rejected
- Read reviews carefully (often valuable feedback)
- Revise and submit to another journal
- Don't argue with reviewers (even if wrong - just resubmit elsewhere)
- Thick skin required
```

## Advising Students

### PhD Student Mentorship

```markdown
RECRUITING STUDENTS:

WHAT TO LOOK FOR:
‚úÖ Research interest alignment
‚úÖ Self-motivated (PhD is marathon, not sprint)
‚úÖ Strong fundamentals (math, coding, writing)
‚úÖ Coachable (takes feedback well)
‚ùå Expects hand-holding
‚ùå Just wants "Dr." title (won't finish)

INTERVIEW:
- Discuss research interests (theirs & yours)
- "Why PhD? Why this lab?"
- "Tell me about a challenging project you completed"
- Give realistic preview (hard work, long hours, setbacks)
- Mutual fit assessment

---

PhD MILESTONES (Timeline):

YEAR 1:
- Coursework
- Lab rotations (if applicable)
- Choose advisor by end of year

YEAR 2:
- Finish coursework
- Qualifying exam (written + oral)
- Identify dissertation topic

YEAR 3-4:
- Conduct research
- Publish 1-2 papers
- Proposal defense (dissertation plan)

YEAR 5-6:
- Complete research
- Dissertation writing
- Dissertation defense
- Publish 2-3 more papers
- Apply for postdocs/jobs

---

WEEKLY MEETINGS (1-on-1):

STRUCTURE:
- Student presents progress (30 min)
  * What did you accomplish this week?
  * What challenges did you face?
  * What's the plan for next week?
- Discuss roadblocks, brainstorm solutions
- Review figures, writing
- Set goals for next week

RED FLAGS:
üö© Student avoids meetings
üö© No progress for multiple weeks
üö© Vague updates ("Working on X...")
üö© Mental health decline

‚Üí Address early, don't let issues fester

---

WHEN STUDENTS STRUGGLE:

RESEARCH STALL:
- Break down into smaller tasks (overwhelm ‚Üí specific steps)
- Pair with senior student (peer mentorship)
- Revisit project scope (too ambitious?)

MENTAL HEALTH:
- "I noticed you seem stressed. How are you doing?"
- Refer to university counseling services
- Flexible schedule if needed
- Normalize seeking help ("Many students use counseling")

THINKING OF QUITTING:
- Listen without judgment
- Explore reasons (Is it fixable? Different project? Leave academia?)
- PhD isn't for everyone - Master's is okay too
- Support their decision (don't guilt into staying)
```

## Work-Life Balance (or Lack Thereof)

```markdown
TYPICAL PROFESSOR WORKLOAD:

TEACHING (30-40%):
- Lecture prep (2-3 hours per hour of lecture for new course)
- Grading (exams, papers, projects)
- Office hours
- Student emails

RESEARCH (40-50%):
- Grant writing (constant!)
- Conducting experiments, analyzing data
- Writing papers
- Advising students

SERVICE (10-20%):
- Committees (hiring, curriculum, promotions)
- Peer review (10-20 papers/year)
- Conference organizing

TOTAL HOURS: 50-70 hours/week

---

STRATEGIES TO SURVIVE:

TIME BLOCKING:
- Writing: Monday & Wednesday mornings (protect this time!)
- Teaching prep: Tuesday afternoons
- Meetings: Thursday-Friday
- Email: 3x/day (9am, 1pm, 4pm) - not constantly

LEARN TO SAY NO:
- Committees: 2-3 max (say no to rest)
- Peer review: Limit to 10-15/year
- External requests: "Thank you, but my plate is full"

BOUNDARIES:
- No work after 7pm (family time)
- No work Saturdays (recharge)
- Vacation = actual vacation (not working from beach)

DELEGATE:
- TA can grade exams
- Senior PhD student can mentor junior students
- Research assistant can collect data

EFFICIENCY:
- Recycle content (lectures, slides improve each year)
- Templates (grant budgets, IRB applications)
- Batch tasks (grade all exams in one sitting)
```

## Key Takeaways

1. **Publish or perish** - Research productivity is #1 for tenure
2. **Grant writing is essential** - External funding = job security
3. **Teaching matters** - Don't neglect (student evals count)
4. **Mentor students** - Your success = their success
5. **Protect your time** - Say no, set boundaries, avoid burnout
